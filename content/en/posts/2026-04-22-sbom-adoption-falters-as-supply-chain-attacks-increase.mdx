## Executive Summary
Organizations are failing to translate Software Bill of Materials (SBOM) data into actionable security decisions, according to analysis from SecurityWeek. While SBOM adoption has increased, a governance and intelligence gap is preventing security teams from effectively mitigating the rising tide of software supply chain attacks.

## Technical Analysis
The core problem, as reported by SecurityWeek, is not a lack of SBOM generation but a failure to operationalize the data. Sources indicate approximately 60% of organizations now produce SBOMs, but around 70% of security teams report significant difficulty using them to make timely risk decisions. The primary obstacle is the overwhelming volume and complexity of component data, which lacks the contextual intelligence needed to prioritize remediation. The article identifies a missing "governance-driven intelligence layer" required to correlate SBOM data with Vulnerability Exploitability eXchange (VEX) advisories and other threat intelligence.

## Threat Actor Context
The source material does not attribute the rise in supply chain attacks to a specific threat actor or group. It frames the issue as a systemic weakness in defensive tooling and processes that attackers broadly exploit.

## Mitigations & Recommendations
The analysis recommends moving beyond basic SBOM generation to implement a structured governance framework. This involves integrating SBOM and VEX data with continuous threat intelligence to create explainable risk scores for components. The goal is to shift from unmanageable data aggregation to intelligence-driven decision-making that clearly identifies which vulnerabilities in the software supply chain require immediate action.
