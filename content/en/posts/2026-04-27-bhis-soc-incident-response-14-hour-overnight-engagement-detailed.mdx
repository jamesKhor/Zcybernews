## Executive Summary

Black Hills Information Security (BHIS) published a first-person account of a 14-hour overnight incident response engagement conducted by its SOC team during the Wild West Hackin' Fest conference in Deadwood, South Dakota. The narrative, titled "When the SOC Goes to Deadwood: A Night to Remember," details real-world triage, containment, and communication challenges faced by the team. The account serves as a case study for SOC practitioners, emphasizing the importance of preparation, teamwork, and adaptability under pressure. No specific victim, threat actor, or technical indicators were disclosed.

## Technical Analysis

The BHIS SOC team responded to an active incident that unfolded over a 14-hour overnight period. According to the source, the engagement involved standard incident response phases: detection, analysis, containment, eradication, and recovery. The team operated from the conference venue, coordinating remotely with the affected organization. The narrative highlights the unpredictable nature of incident response, including the need to make rapid decisions with incomplete data. The team faced challenges such as maintaining communication across time zones, managing fatigue, and adapting to evolving threat activity. The account does not specify the type of compromise (e.g., ransomware, data exfiltration) or the tools used, but it underscores the value of rehearsed playbooks and cross-functional collaboration.

## Mitigations & Recommendations

While the BHIS account does not prescribe specific mitigations, it implicitly reinforces several best practices for SOC teams. Organizations should ensure incident response plans are exercised regularly, including after-hours scenarios. Teams should establish clear communication channels and escalation paths before an incident occurs. Fatigue management, such as rotating personnel during extended engagements, is critical. Defenders should also maintain access to remote response tools and pre-authorized containment procedures to minimize decision-making delays during active threats.
