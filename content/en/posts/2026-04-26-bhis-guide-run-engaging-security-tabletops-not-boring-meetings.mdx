## Executive Summary

Black Hills Information Security (BHIS) published a practical guide on April 24, 2026, explaining how security teams can move beyond passive, meeting-style tabletop exercises and adopt immersive scenario-based simulations. The post argues that traditional tabletops often devolve into slide-deck reviews rather than testing actual decision-making under pressure. BHIS recommends structured injects, role-playing, and time constraints to mirror real incident conditions.

## Technical Analysis

The BHIS guide frames tabletop exercises as a low-cost method to validate incident response (IR) plans without deploying live malware or disrupting production systems. Key recommendations include:

- **Scenario design:** Use realistic threat profiles (e.g., ransomware encryption, supply chain compromise, insider data exfiltration) rather than generic “breach” scenarios. BHIS suggests basing scenarios on recent industry incidents or threat actor TTPs.
- **Inject pacing:** Introduce new events (e.g., “the attacker just exfiltrated 500 GB to a cloud storage bucket”) every 10–15 minutes to force teams to triage and communicate under time pressure.
- **Role assignments:** Designate a facilitator, a “red cell” (adversary injector), and observers who track decisions without intervening. BHIS notes that mixing technical and executive roles (CISO, legal, PR) reveals coordination gaps.
- **Outcome measurement:** Score exercises on decision quality, communication speed, and adherence to the IR plan—not on whether the team “stopped” the attack. BHIS emphasizes that the goal is process improvement, not winning.

The guide explicitly warns against common pitfalls: over-explaining the scenario upfront (which removes the surprise element), allowing participants to “phone a friend” outside the room, and failing to debrief with a formal after-action report.

## Mitigations & Recommendations

Security teams should treat tabletops as a recurring, low-stakes testing mechanism—not a one-off compliance checkbox. BHIS recommends scheduling at least two exercises per year, each targeting a different attack vector. Defenders should document decision points, communication breakdowns, and missing tooling during the exercise, then prioritize fixes in the next sprint cycle. For organizations without dedicated IR teams, BHIS suggests using free scenario templates (linked in the post) and inviting cross-functional stakeholders (IT, legal, communications) to build muscle memory before a real incident.
