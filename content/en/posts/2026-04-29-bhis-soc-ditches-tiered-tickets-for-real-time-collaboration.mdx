## Executive Summary

Black Hills Information Security (BHIS) has restructured its Security Operations Center (SOC) to eliminate traditional tiered ticket queues in favor of a flat, collaborative model where analysts work incidents from detection to resolution without escalation handoffs. In an interview published April 29, 2026, BHIS SOC analyst Hayden Covington described the operational shift as a move toward agility and real-time response, replacing the conventional "tier 1, tier 2, tier 3" hierarchy with a team-based approach that aims to reduce mean time to respond (MTTR) and improve analyst engagement.

## Technical Analysis

Covington explained that the traditional SOC model—where junior analysts triage alerts and escalate complex cases up the chain—often introduces latency and context loss during handoffs. BHIS instead adopted a structure where each analyst is empowered to see an incident through from initial alert to closure, supported by peer collaboration rather than formal escalation paths. "Analysts don't escalate tickets, they solve them," Covington said in the interview. The approach relies on real-time communication channels and shared visibility across detection tools, enabling analysts to pool expertise without waiting for a higher-tier assignment.

While Covington did not disclose specific tools or metrics in the published interview, the model mirrors trends in SOC modernization that prioritize cross-training and reduced process overhead. The BHIS SOC handles a range of client environments, and the flat structure is designed to adapt quickly to varying threat landscapes without bureaucratic friction.

## Mitigations & Recommendations

Organizations evaluating SOC models should consider whether tiered queues introduce unnecessary delay in their specific threat profile. For teams with high alert volumes and limited senior staff, a flat model may require investment in automation to handle low-fidelity alerts before they reach analysts. BHIS's approach underscores the value of enabling analysts with broad authority and collaborative tools rather than rigid process gates.
