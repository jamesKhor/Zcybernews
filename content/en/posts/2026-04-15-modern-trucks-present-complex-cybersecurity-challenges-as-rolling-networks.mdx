## Executive Summary
The commercial transportation sector is confronting a fundamental shift in its cybersecurity posture, as modern trucks have effectively become complex, internet-connected networks on wheels. This transformation, driven by telematics, electronic logging devices (ELDs), and a proliferation of sensors, has dramatically expanded the attack surface for threat actors targeting critical supply chains. Industry leaders, convened at the National Motor Freight Traffic Association (NMFTA) Cybersecurity Conference, are now prioritizing the development of tailored security frameworks to mitigate risks ranging from cargo theft to fleet-wide disruptions.

## Technical Analysis
Modern Class 8 trucks and other commercial vehicles are no longer simple mechanical systems. According to discussions at the NMFTA conference, a single truck can contain over 100 electronic control units (ECUs) managing functions from engine performance and braking to climate control and cargo monitoring. These systems are increasingly interconnected via internal controller area network (CAN) buses and external communications channels, including cellular telematics, Wi-Fi, and Bluetooth.

The primary technical concern is the convergence of once-isolated operational technology (OT) with standard information technology (IT) networks. Telematics gateways and ELDs, which are federally mandated for hours-of-service tracking, serve as bridges between the vehicle's internal networks and external cloud services. Security researchers have repeatedly demonstrated that vulnerabilities in these gateway devices or in associated mobile applications can provide a pivot point to critical vehicle systems. While specific, publicly disclosed CVEs for trucking systems are not detailed in the source material, the architectural vulnerability is clear: a compromised infotainment system or a malicious firmware update delivered over-the-air (OTA) could potentially be leveraged to affect safety-critical functions.

## Tactics, Techniques & Procedures
The source material does not detail specific active campaigns but outlines plausible attack vectors based on the technology stack. Potential TTPs include:
*   **Initial Access:** Phishing campaigns targeting fleet management personnel to steal credentials for telematics platforms, or physical access to diagnostic ports (e.g., J1939 or OBD-II) in truck yards.
*   **Execution & Persistence:** Deployment of malicious firmware updates to telematics units or ECUs, or the use of compromised third-party maintenance software.
*   **Impact:** Data theft from cargo manifests and logistics platforms, manipulation of vehicle routing to facilitate cargo theft, or denial-of-service attacks on vehicle systems to cause operational disruption.
The lack of uniform logging and monitoring across these mixed OT/IT environments makes detection and forensic analysis particularly challenging.

## Threat Actor Context
The threat landscape is diverse. Potential actors include financially motivated cybercriminals seeking to hijack high-value shipments, state-sponsored groups aiming to disrupt economic activity or steal intellectual property embedded in supply chain data, and insider threats. The transportation sector's role as a backbone of critical infrastructure makes it a high-value target for actors seeking widespread economic impact.

## Mitigations & Recommendations
Industry efforts highlighted at the NMFTA conference focus on building resilience through collaboration and standardization. Key recommendations include:
*   **Segmentation:** Implementing strong network segmentation to isolate critical vehicle control systems from non-essential entertainment and telematics networks.
*   **Supply Chain Security:** Demanding greater cybersecurity transparency from component manufacturers and software suppliers, including adherence to emerging standards like ISO/SAE 21434 for road vehicle cybersecurity engineering.
*   **Unified Monitoring:** Developing shared threat intelligence and best practices for monitoring mixed-fleet environments, as many carriers operate vehicles from multiple manufacturers with disparate systems.
*   **Incident Response Planning:** Creating sector-specific playbooks for responding to cyber incidents that affect vehicle operations, including coordination with law enforcement and government agencies like CISA.
