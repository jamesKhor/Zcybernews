## Executive Summary

The 2026 threat landscape is defined not by novel attacker techniques but by the accelerating speed at which adversaries exploit weak credentials and known vulnerabilities, outpacing defenders' ability to respond, according to Christiaan Beek, VP of Threat Analytics at Rapid7. In a recent episode of the firm's "Experts on Experts" podcast, Beek highlighted that the core challenge for organizations is no longer awareness of threats but the operational tempo of remediation.

## Technical Analysis

Beek drew on recent research from Rapid7 Labs to illustrate that attackers are weaponizing publicly available exploits and credential dumps faster than many enterprise patching cycles can accommodate. The conversation emphasized that the majority of successful intrusions observed by Rapid7 still originate from unpatched, known vulnerabilities — not zero-days — combined with weak or reused credentials. The shift in 2026 is the compression of the window between disclosure and exploitation. Where defenders historically had weeks to patch, adversaries now operationalize exploits within days or hours. Beek noted that this velocity places a premium on asset inventory accuracy, automated patch deployment, and real-time credential monitoring. Rapid7 did not release specific CVE IDs or CVSS scores in the podcast, focusing instead on broad trends.

## Mitigations & Recommendations

Defenders should prioritize reducing the time-to-patch for known exploited vulnerabilities, particularly those listed in CISA's Known Exploited Vulnerabilities (KEV) catalog. Implementing automated credential rotation and enforcing multi-factor authentication (MFA) on all internet-facing services can blunt the most common initial access vectors. Organizations should also audit their asset inventory to ensure no shadow IT systems remain unpatched. Rapid7's analysis suggests that improving detection and response speed — not just tooling — is the primary lever for closing the gap with attackers in 2026.
