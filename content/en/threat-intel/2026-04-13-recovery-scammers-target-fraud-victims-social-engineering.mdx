## Executive Summary
Individuals who have recently reported financial fraud to authorities are being systematically targeted in sophisticated secondary scams. According to ESET research, criminals obtain victim details from initial fraud incidents—often via compromised law enforcement or financial institution portals—and pose as recovery agents to steal more money. This attack chain exploits the emotional vulnerability and urgency of victims, making the social engineering highly effective and financially damaging.

## Technical Analysis
The technical execution of these recovery scams relies heavily on information gathering and impersonation rather than malware or technical exploits. The primary initial access vector is the compromise of data from the first fraud event. ESET analysts note that scammers likely acquire victim details, including names, contact information, and specifics of their losses, from sources such as unsecured law enforcement report databases, leaked information from fraudulent initial schemes, or data brokers trading in "sucker lists." The attackers then use this data to craft highly personalized phishing communications via phone, email, or social media. The communications often spoof legitimate entities like the FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3), the Federal Trade Commission (FTC), or fake law firms. The technical sophistication lies in the contextual accuracy of the communication, which references real case details to build immediate credibility.

## Indicators of Compromise
None identified at this time.

## Tactics, Techniques & Procedures
The threat actors employ a consistent set of tactics aligned with the social engineering phase of the MITRE ATT&CK framework.
*   **Reconnaissance (TA0043):** Gathering victim information from previous fraud reports or purchased lead lists.
*   **Resource Development (TA0042):** Creating fake websites, social media profiles, and documentation for impersonated recovery agencies or law firms.
*   **Initial Access (TA0001):** Using communication channels like phone calls (vishing) or email (phishing) to establish contact, leveraging detailed victim information to bypass skepticism.
*   **Execution (TA0002):** Manipulating the victim into executing financial transactions, often under the guise of paying "refundable" fees, taxes, or bonds required to release their lost funds.
*   **Impact (TA0040):** Causing direct financial loss through wire transfers, cryptocurrency payments, or gift card purchases.
A key procedure is the use of urgency and authority, pressuring victims to act quickly before their "window" for recovery closes, and instructing them to keep the process secret from their bank or family.

## Threat Actor Context
The actors behind these schemes are financially motivated criminal groups, likely overlapping with or connected to the perpetrators of the initial frauds. ESET suggests the existence of a criminal ecosystem where victim data is a commodity, sold or shared among groups specializing in different fraud types. The origin of these groups is unclear from the available reporting, but their operations are global, targeting English-speaking victims primarily in the US, Canada, Australia, and the UK. Their business model is opportunistic, focusing on a proven high-conversion target demographic: individuals who have already demonstrated a susceptibility to fraud and are desperate for a resolution.

## Detection & Hunting Queries
Detection focuses on anomalous communications and financial red flags rather than endpoint telemetry.
*   **Communication Monitoring:** Hunt for emails or calls claiming to be from government agencies (IC3, FTC, Secret Service) offering fund recovery services. Legitimate agencies do not initiate contact in this manner.
*   **Financial Pattern Analysis:** Flag transactions where the narrative or memo line includes terms like "recovery fee," "tax bond," "unlock fee," or "government processing charge" directed to unknown individuals or entities.
*   **Data Leak Hunting:** Organizations handling fraud reports should audit access logs for victim data portals to detect suspicious queries or bulk downloads that could indicate a credential compromise or insider threat.

## Mitigations & Recommendations
Mitigation is primarily awareness-based for potential victims, with specific actions for organizations handling sensitive fraud data.
For Individuals:
*   Assume any unsolicited contact promising to recover lost funds is a scam. Legitimate government agencies will not call, email, or text you offering this service.
*   Never pay upfront fees for recovery services. This is a hallmark of fraud.
*   Verify contacts independently. If someone claims to be from an agency, hang up and call the official number listed on the agency's genuine .gov website.
*   Be wary of anyone urging secrecy or immediate action.

For Organizations (Law Enforcement, Financial Institutions):
*   Secure portals containing fraud reports with strong access controls, multi-factor authentication, and audit logging.
*   Educate staff who interact with victims about this secondary scam trend so they can issue explicit warnings.
*   Include clear, prominent disclaimers on public-facing report submission pages stating that the agency will never initiate contact to solicit fees for recovery.
