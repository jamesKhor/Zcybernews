## Executive Summary

Threat actors are now distributing structured operational security (OPSEC) playbooks that detail layered infrastructure management, identity separation, and long-term evasion tactics, according to research published by Flare. These guides, observed across underground forums, codify best practices for avoiding detection by defenders, including specific steps for compartmentalizing command-and-control (C2) nodes, rotating credentials, and sanitizing digital fingerprints. The emergence of formalized OPSEC playbooks signals a maturation in adversary tradecraft, moving from ad-hoc evasion to repeatable, documented procedures.

## Technical Analysis

Flare's analysis, reported by BleepingComputer, describes these playbooks as covering multiple phases of an operation. Key components include:

- **Infrastructure layering:** Playbooks recommend using multiple tiers of proxies, VPNs, and residential IP networks to obfuscate the origin of C2 traffic. Specific guidance includes avoiding direct connections from known hosting providers and instead routing through compromised residential routers or IoT devices.
- **Identity separation:** Adversaries are advised to maintain distinct digital personas for each operation, including separate email accounts, cryptocurrency wallets, and virtual machine environments. The playbooks emphasize never reusing usernames or handles across different campaigns to prevent cross-attribution.
- **Long-term evasion:** Techniques such as staggered beaconing intervals, jittered traffic patterns, and periodic infrastructure teardown are documented to avoid pattern-based detection. Some playbooks include counter-forensic steps like wiping logs, disabling telemetry on compromised hosts, and using encrypted communication channels that mimic legitimate application traffic.

The playbooks are not attributed to a single group but appear across multiple forums, suggesting a broader trend of knowledge-sharing within the criminal ecosystem. Flare notes that the level of detail rivals internal security documentation from legitimate organizations.

## Mitigations & Recommendations

Defenders should monitor for infrastructure patterns that align with these playbook recommendations, such as traffic routing through multiple residential proxies or unusual beaconing behavior that deliberately avoids regular intervals. Network detection teams can focus on identifying C2 traffic that mimics legitimate application protocols (e.g., HTTPS with non-standard certificate chains). Identity-based detection — correlating usernames, email addresses, or wallet addresses across incidents — may become less reliable as adversaries adopt stricter compartmentalization. Organizations should also review their own OPSEC practices to ensure that internal security documentation does not leak or become accessible to threat actors.
