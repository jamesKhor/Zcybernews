## Executive Summary

Weekly cyberattack volumes targeting African organizations dropped 22% over the past year, according to telemetry analyzed by Dark Reading. The decline correlates with an apparent pivot by threat actors toward Latin American targets, though the report does not attribute the shift to any specific group or campaign. The data underscores how regional attack patterns can shift rapidly as adversaries rebalance operational priorities.

## Technical Analysis

Dark Reading's analysis, based on aggregated intrusion-detection and firewall-log data from multiple unnamed sources, found that African organizations experienced a sustained reduction in weekly attack attempts. The 22% decline stands in contrast to prior years when Africa saw some of the highest growth rates in cyberattack volume globally. The report explicitly notes that the drop does not reflect an overall decrease in global malicious activity; rather, it indicates a geographic redistribution of targeting. Latin America has absorbed a larger share of the attack volume, though the report provides no specific percentage increase for that region. The shift may stem from changes in attacker infrastructure deployment, victim-sector opportunity, or law-enforcement pressure in certain African jurisdictions, but Dark Reading does not offer a definitive causal explanation.

## Mitigations & Recommendations

Defenders in African organizations should not interpret the reported decline as a signal to reduce security posture. Attack-volume data is inherently noisy and can mask targeted operations. Organizations in both Africa and Latin America should maintain robust monitoring for lateral movement and credential-based attacks, which remain common regardless of regional volume trends. Cross-regional threat-intelligence sharing can help defenders anticipate shifts as attackers relocate infrastructure.
